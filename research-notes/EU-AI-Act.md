Research Notes: EU AI Act

What is the EU AI Act?

- The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive legal framework for Artificial Intelligence.
- It aims to ensure that AI systems are safe, trustworthy, and respectful of fundamental rights.
- The Act introduces a risk-based approach to AI regulation.
- It imposes obligations on AI developers and deployers depending on the level of risk associated with the AI system.

---

Why Was It Created?

- To protect citizens from harmful uses of AI.
- To ensure AI respects fundamental rights and democratic values.
- To increase trust in AI technologies.
- To provide legal certainty for organizations developing and deploying AI systems.
- To create a harmonized regulatory framework across the European Union.

---

Risk Categories

Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited)

AI systems that pose a clear threat to people's safety, rights, or livelihoods.

Examples:

- Social scoring systems
- Subliminal manipulation
- Certain forms of biometric categorization
- Systems exploiting vulnerable groups

Status:

- Banned under the EU AI Act

---

High Risk (Strictly Regulated)

AI systems used in critical sectors where decisions significantly affect individuals.

Examples:

- Healthcare
- Education
- Employment and recruitment
- Law enforcement
- Critical infrastructure

Requirements:

- Risk assessment
- Documentation
- Human oversight
- Transparency
- Compliance monitoring

Example:

- AI systems used for CV ranking and candidate evaluation

---

Limited Risk (Transparency Obligations)

AI systems that require users to be informed when they interact with AI.

Examples:

- AI chatbots
- Deepfakes
- AI-generated content

Requirements:

- Transparency obligations
- User notification

---

Minimal Risk (Little or No Regulation)

Low-risk AI applications with limited impact on rights and safety.

Examples:

- Spam filters
- Video game AI
- Recommendation systems

Requirements:

- Generally exempt from additional obligations

---

General-Purpose AI Models (GPAI)

- Includes powerful foundation models.
- Developers must comply with transparency requirements.
- Copyright obligations must be respected.
- Providers must publish summaries regarding training data and content.

---

Who Is Affected?

- Organizations developing AI systems.
- Organizations deploying AI systems.
- Companies selling AI products in the EU market.
- Global companies whose AI systems affect individuals residing within the European Union.

---

Key Takeaway

The EU AI Act differs from OECD and NIST because it is a legally binding regulatory framework rather than a set of principles or risk-management guidelines. Its risk-based approach attempts to balance innovation with the protection of fundamental rights and public trust.
